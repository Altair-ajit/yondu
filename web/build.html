MULTI-FREQUENCY BASED CONTROLLER · BUILD GUIDE

Build your own arrow.

Whistle. Hum. Strike a note. With about $100 of parts you can turn any audible cue into a machine command — a configurable controller that hears frequencies and emits instructions to whatever you point it at. This guide walks you through the whole build. The worked example throughout is the drum-driven rover Team Yondu ran at the UMD Capstone Design Expo; swap in your own actuator and the recipe doesn't change.

00 What you're building

Two halves. The transmitter lives near the sound source: an INMP441 I2S microphone feeding an ESP32 that streams raw audio over Bluetooth. The receiver rides on the machine you want to control: a Raspberry Pi 4 that FFTs the stream, matches frequency peaks against a JSON map you write, and pushes command codes out a serial port. What listens on that port is up to you — in the rover example it's an Arduino UNO driving two DC motors. Budget your latency and you'll get sound-to-action in under 200 ms.

On the Pi, your signal path runs: 1. convert the raw I2S stream to dB SPL · 2. band-pass filter to your sound source's range · 3. subtract the RMS-estimated noise floor · 4. FFT to isolate frequency peaks · 5. group peaks within ~5 Hz / 5 dB to cut processing overhead · then map the surviving peaks to output codes from your configuration file.

03 Firmware

Transmitter (ESP32)

Flash hardware/esp32/yondu_mic_tx.ino from the Arduino IDE (board: ESP32 Dev Module). It reads 512-sample I2S buffers at 48 kHz/32-bit and streams every sample as a text line over Bluetooth serial, advertising as ESP32_Yondu. The I2S mic bring-up follows atomic14's esp32-i2s-mic-test. Nothing here is instrument- or actuator-specific — flash it and forget it.

Example actuator — the rover sketch

Flash hardware/arduino/yondu_rover.ino (board: Arduino UNO). Treat it as a template for any actuator you build: a readStringUntil('\n') loop that matches incoming codes and fires a routine per code. Worth copying even if you never build the rover — declare pins with #define/const (no dynamic allocation), call your stop routine first thing in setup(), and default outputs LOW so a brown-out can't twitch the hardware. The rover maps its codes like this:

CodeRover actionPWM
ADrive forward128
BDrive backward128
CPivot right100
DPivot left128
0Stop0
Run your turns slow. Detection arrives in ~20 ms windows, so a fast pivot overshoots before the next command lands. Dropping the turn PWM makes each note nudge the heading a controllable amount — this one change is most of why expo visitors could actually steer the rover.

04 Receiver setup (Raspberry Pi)

# dependencies
sudo apt update && sudo apt install python3-numpy python3-serial bluez

# pair with your transmitter (once)
bluetoothctl
  scan on          # note ESP32_Yondu's MAC
  pair <MAC>
  trust <MAC>
  quit

# bind the Bluetooth serial port (each boot, or via systemd)
sudo rfcomm bind /dev/rfcomm0 <MAC>

# drop in your config and run
cp config.example.json config.json
python3 signal_processor.py

signal_processor.py buffers 1024-sample windows, applies a Hanning window + FFT, converts magnitudes to dB, and checks every peak in your config: frequency within sensitivity Hz and amplitude above threshold → its output_code goes out the serial port. Nothing matched → 0, which your actuator should treat as "stand down."

05 Calibrate & map — make it yours

Here's where the system becomes a general control surface: the Pi never hardcodes a note. It reads a JSON file from the USB stick mapping frequency bands to whatever output codes you choose. New instrument, new room, new machine — you edit a file, not code.

Step 1 — survey your sound source

Run the processor with the env_scan config: instead of emitting commands, it prints the top-20 FFT peaks it hears. Play each note you plan to use — repeatedly, at different angles and intensities — and log which frequencies recur and how much they wander. 

{ "config_name": "env_scan", "Global_Sensitivity": 10.0,
  "Global_Amplitude": 65.0, "Peaks": {} }

Step 2 — pick separable frequencies

Keep the notes whose measured bands don't overlap. Adjacent or harmonically related pitches will cross-trigger; the rover build tested every drum pad and kept the four most stable, well-separated ones.

Step 3 — write your map

{
  "config_name": "my_controller",
  "Global_Sensitivity": 10.0,   // default ± Hz tolerance
  "Global_Amplitude": 65.0,     // dB threshold — raise it in loud rooms
  "Peaks": {
    "Peak1": { "frequency": 440.00, "sensitivity": 5.0, "output_code": "A" },
    "Peak2": { "frequency": 523.25, "sensitivity": 5.0, "output_code": "B" },
    "Peak3": { "frequency": 587.33, "sensitivity": 5.0, "output_code": "C" },
    "Peak4": { "frequency": 659.25, "sensitivity": 5.0, "output_code": "D" }
  }
}

The output codes are just bytes on a serial line — point them at anything. Tone-triggered lights, appliances, or locks in a smart home. A factory rig that self-disengages when the frequencies of a failing bearing appear (there, the "instrument" is the machine itself). An exhibit visitors play notes at to trigger displays. Swap the rover sketch's motor routines for whatever your build needs; the acquisition and decision layers never change.

Placement & noise, before you blame the code: don't mount the mic inside a resonant body (on the original build it amplified voice frequencies into false triggers — keep it near the instrument), trigger one note at a time (simultaneous tones interfere and bury the dominant peak), and rehearse with background noise playing before you trust your map in a real room.

06 Pitch control — run the simulator's mode on your hardware

Discrete notes (§05) are one map. The simulator demonstrates the other: pitch-style continuous control, where the controller calibrates to your whistle and reads where your pitch sits inside your own range — hold your center note to go, slide high to turn one way, low to turn the other, go quiet to stop. You can run that same scheme on this hardware without touching a line of code.

How the mapping works

The whistle print you download from the simulator doesn't store three narrow notes — its bands tile your entire calibrated range, split at the same dead-zone boundaries the sim's gesture classifier uses (±22% of your range in log-frequency, around your center). Any whistle inside your range therefore always lands in exactly one band:

You whistle…BandCodeRoverSim arrow
near your center noteHoldAdrive forwardthrust
above the dead zonePitchUpCpivot rightbank right
below the dead zonePitchDownDpivot leftbank left
…nothing (silence)—0stopcoast

Setup, end to end

  1. Open the simulator, enable your microphone, and run the ~8-second calibration (noise floor → steady note → sweep up → sweep down).
  2. Fly the arrow until the controls feel natural — your hardware will obey the identical gesture boundaries.
  3. Hit ⇩ whistle print and save the JSON.
  4. Rename it config.json, drop it on your USB config stick, and start signal_processor.py (§04).
  5. Whistle. The arrow on your screen and the machine on your floor are now speaking the same language.
Tune for whistles: the rover example band-passes ~20 Hz–1 kHz for the drum; whistles live around 500–3000 Hz, so widen your filter range accordingly. In loud rooms, raise Global_Amplitude so breaths and chatter stay below the gate.
What's different from the sim: the stock rover firmware drives at fixed PWM, so whistle loudness doesn't scale speed, and turns are discrete pivots at the detection cadence rather than smooth banking — that's exactly why §03 runs turn PWM slow. If you want the full demo feel, a small mod to signal_processor.py can scale an extra PWM byte from the detected peak's amplitude and pass it alongside the code.
